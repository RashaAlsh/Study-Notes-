## What is an Artifact?

In Scrum, **Artifacts represent work or value**.

Artifacts are designed to **maximize transparency of information** so that everyone involved has the same understanding of the work and the current state of the product.

Key ideas:

- Scrum uses **standard artifacts** to ensure a shared understanding.
- Decisions about **value optimization and risk control** are based on the state of the artifacts.
- Each artifact includes a **commitment**.

---

# Product Backlog

The **Product Backlog** is an **ordered list of everything that might be needed in the product**.

It acts as the **single source of requirements** for the product.

The **Product Owner** is responsible for managing the Product Backlog.

## Characteristics of the Product Backlog

The Product Backlog contains:

- Product **features**
- **Functions**
- **Requirements**
- **Bug fixes**
- **Alternatives**
- **Non-functional requirements**

Other characteristics:

- Items are **ordered** based on factors such as value, effort, dependencies, and risk.
- Higher-priority **Product Backlog Items (PBIs)** are **more detailed and refined**.
- The Product Backlog is **never complete**; it continuously evolves.
- Each PBI typically contains:
  - Description
  - Order
  - Estimate
  - Value

### Commitment

The commitment for the **Product Backlog** is the **Product Goal**.

---

# Sprint Backlog

The **Sprint Backlog** is the set of **Product Backlog Items selected for the Sprint**, plus a **plan to deliver the product Increment and achieve the Sprint Goal**.

The **Developers** are responsible for the Sprint Backlog.

It serves as a **forecast of the work** that will be completed during the Sprint.

The Sprint Backlog makes **all necessary work visible** so the team can achieve the Sprint Goal.

## Important Notes

- Developers **volunteer for work**; work is not assigned to individuals.
- Developers can **add, remove, or change Sprint Backlog items** during the Sprint.
- Changes are allowed **as long as the Sprint Goal remains intact**.
- Work is **re-estimated and updated regularly**, often daily.

### Commitment

The commitment for the **Sprint Backlog** is the **Sprint Goal**.

# Progress Tracking

Scrum uses the **Inspect and Adapt principle** to track progress regularly.

Progress can be tracked using tools such as:

- **Product Burndown Chart**
- **Sprint Burndown Chart**

These charts are **not required by the Scrum Guide**, but they are commonly used in practice.

## Burndown Chart

A **Burndown Chart** shows:

- **Work remaining**
- Compared against **time**

Purpose:

- Predict when work will be completed
- Estimate the likelihood of finishing on time

Important reminder:

Scrum follows **empiricism**, meaning decisions should be based on **observations and past results**.

# The Increment

The **Increment** is the **sum of all Product Backlog items completed during a Sprint**, combined with increments from previous Sprints.

At the end of a Sprint:

- The new Increment must be **“Done”**
- It must be **usable**
- It must meet the team’s **Definition of Done**

The Increment must always be **in a releasable state**, even if the Product Owner chooses not to release it yet.

### Commitment

The commitment for the **Increment** is the **Definition of Done**.

---

# Definition of Done

Sometimes work planned for a Sprint is **not fully completed**.

Incomplete work is called **“Undone” work**.

Undone work causes several problems:

- Reduced **predictability**
- **Unreliable information**
- Loss of **stakeholder trust**
- Broken **development rhythm**
- Inability to measure **velocity**
- The increment becomes **not shippable**
- Reduced **transparency**

## Preventing Undone Work

To prevent these problems, Scrum Teams use a **Definition of Done**.

The **Definition of Done** is a **shared understanding of when work on a product increment is complete**.

It ensures:

- Consistent quality
- Transparency
- Reliable progress tracking

### Commitment

The **Definition of Done** is the commitment associated with the **Increment**.
