# Organizational Controls (ISO/IEC 27002:2022)

Organizational controls represent the **governance layer** of information security. They are not technical tools; instead, they are **rules, processes, and structures** that embed security into the company's culture and operations.

---

## 1. Policies and Frameworks

The foundation of organizational security—without policies, there is no mandate for action.

- **Information Security Policy**  
  A high-level document approved by management, stating the organization's commitment to security.

- **Topic-Specific Policies**  
  Detailed rules for specific areas, e.g., access control, clear desk/clear screen, mobile device usage.

- **ISMS (Information Security Management System)**  
  Uses the PDCA cycle to organize and maintain policies and procedures.

---

## 2. Roles and Responsibilities

Security is everyone's job, but accountability must be assigned.

- **Assignment of Responsibilities**  
  Clearly define and assign security roles (e.g., CISO, Asset Owner, System Administrator).

- **Segregation of Duties**  
  Split conflicting duties to reduce risk of fraud or error.  
  *Example:* The person requesting a payment should not be the one approving it.

---

## 3. Asset Management

"You cannot protect what you do not know you have."

- **Inventory of Assets**  
  Maintain a list of all information assets (data, software, hardware).

- **Ownership**  
  Assign an owner for each asset responsible for classification and protection.

- **Acceptable Use**  
  Define how employees may use company assets.  
  *Example:* No personal use of work laptops for gambling.

---

## 4. Relations with Authorities and Special Interest Groups

Organizations operate within broader legal and professional ecosystems.

- **Contact with Authorities**  
  Procedures for reporting incidents to police, regulators, or government bodies.

- **Special Interest Groups**  
  Participation in security forums or professional organizations (e.g., ISACA, CIS) to stay informed on threats and best practices.

---

## 5. Supplier Relationships

Third-party vendors often introduce new risks.

- **Information Security in Supplier Relationships**  
  Security requirements must be written into contracts.

- **Monitoring and Review**  
  Regularly verify that suppliers comply with agreed-upon security standards.

---

## 6. Information Security in Project Management

**Security by Design:** integrate security from the beginning of every new project rather than adding it later.

---

## Exam Focus
